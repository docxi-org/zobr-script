## Executive Summary

The global debate over AI safety regulation is entering a critical inflection point in 2026. The world's three major regulatory blocs — the EU (strict comprehensive rules), the US (federal deregulation with state-level fragmentation), and China (sectoral state-directed control) — are diverging rather than converging. Meanwhile, frontier AI companies have become the dominant political actors, spending unprecedented sums to shape the rules governing their own industry. The core tension is not simply "innovation vs. safety" — it is about who gets to define what counts as safe, and who bears the cost if they are wrong.

## 2. Regulatory Frameworks

### EU AI Act (2024–2027)

The world's first comprehensive binding AI law, passed in 2024:

- **Feb 2025**: Prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI (social scoring, real-time biometrics) took effect
- **Aug 2025**: Governance rules + General Purpose AI (GPAI) model obligations activated; AI Office and AI Board operational
- **Aug 2026**: Main enforcement begins; high-risk AI systems (Annex III) must be compliant
- **Proposed delay (Digital Omnibus, Nov 2025)**: Commission proposed pushing Annex III deadline to Dec 2027 and Annex I/products to Aug 2028, conditional on harmonized standards availability

**Penalties**: Up to €35M or 7% global turnover (prohibited practices); €15M or 3% (most violations).

### US Federal Framework (Post-Trump EO, Dec 2025)

- Biden's Executive Order on AI (Oct 2023) revoked by Trump (Jan 2025)
- December 2025 Trump EO: establishes national AI policy framework, aims to preempt state laws, emphasizes "minimally burdensome" regulation
- No overarching federal AI law exists; reliance on sector-specific guidance
- Senate vote **99-1** to strip 10-year moratorium on state AI enforcement from reconciliation bill — states retain power

### China (Iterative Sectoral Approach)

Between 2021–2025, China enacted more sector-specific AI regulations than any other country:
- Algorithm recommendation rules → deepfake rules → generative AI rules
- October 2025: Cybersecurity Law amendments bring AI explicitly into national law (effective Jan 2026): algorithm R&D support, training data infrastructure, AI ethics rulemaking, risk assessment

### International (Paris AI Action Summit, Feb 2025)

- 1,000+ participants from 100+ countries at Grand Palais, Paris
- Co-chaired by Macron and Modi
- **Final Statement**: Signed by 60+ nations including China, India, EU, African Union — focused on "inclusive and sustainable AI"
- **Notable absentees**: US and UK refused to sign
- France pledged $400M endowment for "Current AI" foundation (open-source tools, public datasets)
- ROOST initiative: Google, Discord, OpenAI, Roblox → open-source child safety tools

## 3. Tensions: Innovation vs. Safety

### 3.1 The Core Trade-off

**Pro-regulation argument**: AI poses novel risks (bias, manipulation, autonomous weapons, catastrophic misuse) that markets cannot self-correct. Binding rules create accountability, enable redress, protect vulnerable populations.

**Anti-regulation argument**: Premature or overly prescriptive regulation stifles innovation, disadvantages domestic industry in the US-China AI race, creates compliance costs that only incumbents can absorb, and may be technically illiterate.

### 3.2 Structural Tensions

**US Federal vs. State**: The most immediate US flashpoint. States are legislating aggressively (CA's landmark frontier AI law; CO, NY, IL). Tech industry spent **$314M lobbying federally** in 9 months of 2025; Meta launched a Super PAC to fight state AI laws. Senate blocked the moratorium — states will keep legislating.

**Open-Source vs. Closed Models**: Regulation focused on powerful closed models (OpenAI, Anthropic) inadvertently advantages open-source (Meta's LLaMA). Meta frames open-source as democratization; critics argue uncontrolled open-source models eliminate accountability.

**Existential Risk vs. Proximate Harms**: The AI safety community (Anthropic, FLI) focuses on long-term catastrophic scenarios. Civil rights organizations and regulators focus on immediate discrimination, surveillance, and labor displacement. These framings imply different regulatory targets.

**National Security vs. Company Ethics**: The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute (March 2026) epitomizes this: the government demands rapid military AI deployment; Anthropic tried to set limits on wartime use of Claude. The outcome exposes the fragility of voluntary safety commitments under government pressure.

**EU Competitiveness**: The Digital Omnibus delay proposal reflects European industry lobbying that strict AI Act enforcement will disadvantage EU companies vs. US and Chinese competitors with no equivalent burden.

## 4. Hidden Interests and Power Dynamics

### The Regulatory Capture Problem

OpenAI and Microsoft advocate for **federal licensing regimes** — framing AI regulation as analogous to nuclear risk management. Critics call this **regulatory capture**: by pushing for high compliance bars that only incumbents can meet, they erect barriers to entry protecting their market position while appearing safety-conscious.

Evidence:
- OpenAI/Microsoft: backed federal licensing, which would require costly compliance their competitors cannot afford
- 500+ firms lobbied US AI policy in 2025, doubling from 2023
- $1 billion+ spent by Big Tech to stop state AI regulation
- 1-in-4 federal lobbyists now work on AI

### The Safety-Washing Risk

Companies that publish safety policies do so voluntarily with no enforcement. The 2025 Future of Life Institute AI Safety Index found **8 major companies have no credible catastrophic risk prevention plans** — yet they publish "Responsible Scaling Policies" and "Constitutional AI" frameworks used partly as lobbying props.

### The China Factor

China's AI governance serves dual purposes: **controlling content and political risk** (primary goal) and **supporting national AI champions** (secondary). China's regulatory posture is often misread in the West as "safety regulation" — it is primarily about state control and information sovereignty, not the epistemic or physical safety concerns driving EU/US debate.

### EU Industry Capture

The proposed Digital Omnibus delay for high-risk AI systems, filed just 15 months after the AI Act's passage, shows industry has successfully lobbied to slow implementation. The "wait for harmonized standards" framing provides regulatory uncertainty that benefits incumbents.

## 5. Where the Debate Is Heading

### Near-term (2026)

- **EU enforcement begins in earnest** (August 2026): Companies must have completed conformity assessments; penalties become real
- **US patchwork deepens**: Without federal law, state AI legislation multiplies; California's frontier AI law is a template
- **China consolidates**: Post-Cybersecurity Law amendments, China has the most coherent (though state-directed) AI governance structure globally
- **International cooperation fragile**: The US/UK abstention from Paris shows the major AI powers cannot agree on governance norms

### Medium-term (2027–2028)

- **EU-US regulatory divergence becomes structural**: European companies face compliance costs US competitors do not; potential for mutual recognition deals or continued friction
- **Compute governance emerges**: Discussion of regulating AI training compute (chip export controls, compute thresholds) as the most tractable intervention point
- **AI incident catalysis**: A major AI-caused harm (election manipulation, critical infrastructure, weapons proliferation) would rapidly accelerate legislative action globally
- **Open-source accountability gap**: As open models (LLaMA, DeepSeek equivalents) approach frontier capability, the "no single responsible party" problem intensifies

### The Structural Dynamic

The debate is shaped by **three irreconcilable forces**:
1. **Geopolitical competition** (US-China AI race) → forces against restriction
2. **Democratic accountability** (harms to real people) → forces for binding rules
3. **Corporate capture** (incumbents shaping rules) → forces toward voluntary frameworks with high barriers to entry

The most likely 2026–2028 outcome: **regulatory fragmentation**, not convergence — EU binding, US voluntary+state patchwork, China state-controlled. This is optimal for large incumbents (who can absorb compliance costs), harmful for smaller players and civil society, and leaves genuinely novel catastrophic risks unaddressed by any framework.

## 6. Open Questions

1. Can any regulatory framework meaningfully govern frontier AI before the technology moves beyond its scope?
2. Will a major AI-caused incident catalyze rapid international cooperation, or deepen the "move fast" vs. "move carefully" division?
3. Is the US federal vs. state tension resolvable? The 99-1 Senate vote suggests not via preemption.
4. How will compute governance (chip export controls) interact with domestic AI regulation?
5. Does the Anthropic-Pentagon dispute signal the end of meaningful corporate AI safety limits under national security pressure?
6. Can open-source AI governance exist at all, given the absence of a responsible deployment party?
